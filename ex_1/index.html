How Street Art Took Off in the Suburbs of Tunis

A portrait of a left-wing Tunisian politician who was killed in 2013. (Thessa Lageman)

Street art is relatively new in this North African country. But over the past few years, more and more colorful pieces of graffiti, “calligraffiti”—a hybrid of Arabic calligraphy and graffiti—and other forms of art have started to appear on the walls of cafes and schools, alongside roads, and even on the domes of mosques. Young street artists aim to brighten up the suburbs and at the same time deliver a message.

How a Beirut graffiti artist is using his murals to try to unite a fragmented city

Halwani’s work in in Gemmayze, depicting Fairouz, a Lebanese singer and cultural icon.

Yazan Halwani is a young Beiruti artist who paints murals of revered Lebanese and Arab figures on prominent walls in a bid to help overcome sectarianism in this fragmented city. “What I try to do,” he explains down a fuzzy line from Beirut, “is write the stories of the city, on its own walls – creating a memory for the city.”
